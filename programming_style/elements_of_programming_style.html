Introduction

This article is taking a different approach than we have before in our other elements of articles. Back in ancient times, probably around 2003-2004, I found Strunk and White’s The Elements of Style. A classic style manual for improving your writing, formulated as non-negotiable rules. The year 2021 will go into history as the year of the P. The year I discovered The Elements of Programming Style by Brian W. Kernighan and P.J. Plauger.

The Elements of Programming Style sets out to do for programming what The Elements of Style did for writing: a set of guidelines to follow when programming, resulting in more readable code (versus more readable prose). In fact, Kernighan and Plauger, refer to The Elements of Style explicitly where they “tried to emulate their brevity by concentrating on the essential practical aspects of style”.

Why are you reading this? Why did I write this? “What a useless endeavor. Even in the grand scheme of useleness, it’s deeply unsettling how useless this is.” I see the reader has a sense of drama. Welcome, you’ve come to the right place. The Elements of Programming Style was first published in 1974 and all code examples of bad style and good style are all in Fortran and PL/I. While interesting historically, this makes the bar for new readers high. This article sets out to do a reading of the original book and chapter by chapter will try come up with modernized examples of Kernighan and Plauger’s rules. I’ll pick [Ruby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language) as the choice of modern language as it’s widely used industry (e.g., at Shopify) and should make sense to a lot of developers living in the year 2020 (and maybe a bit beyond).

A side adventure to this journey, will be finding out how well the original rules have held up: does a modern language like Ruby make it more difficult to violate these rules or are the majority still relevent and in the hands of the skillful developer to apply?

Say what you mean, simply and directly

Let’s start by looking at the following program:

    sig { params(x: Integer, y: Integer, z: Integer).returns(Integer)}
    def self.f(x, y, z)
        if x <= y
            if x <= z
                return x
            end
            return z
        end
        if y <= z
            return y
        end
        z
    end
